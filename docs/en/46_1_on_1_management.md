# 1-ON-1 MEETINGS WITH MANAGEMENT

## Practical English for productive one-on-one meetings with your manager, skip-level leaders, and senior leadership

> **Purpose of this document**
>
> - Navigate 1-on-1 meetings with managers and senior leadership confidently
> - Learn how to prepare, report progress, discuss career growth, and give upward feedback
> - Provide ready-to-use phrases, templates, and full dialogue scripts
> - Help Vietnamese developers communicate professionally in management conversations

> **How to use this document**
>
> - Review relevant sections before your scheduled 1-on-1
> - Adapt phrases to your situation — replace bracketed text with your details
> - Practice the dialogue scripts aloud to build fluency
> - Use the email templates after meetings for follow-up

# 1. PURPOSE OF 1-ON-1 MEETINGS

## 1.1. Why 1-on-1s Matter

Your 1-on-1 with your manager is the most important recurring meeting on your calendar. It is not a status update. It is your dedicated time to align on expectations, get support, and grow your career.

Many Vietnamese developers treat 1-on-1s as optional or use them only to report what they did. This is a missed opportunity. Managers use 1-on-1s to:

- Understand your career aspirations and help you get there
- Remove blockers that slow you down
- Give you feedback you will not hear anywhere else
- Advocate for you in promotion discussions and resource allocation
- Build trust and a working relationship

## 1.2. What Managers Expect from You in 1-on-1s

- You come prepared with topics to discuss
- You are honest about challenges, not just successes
- You propose solutions, not just problems
- You take notes and follow up on action items
- You share your career goals openly

## 1.3. Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Staying silent and letting the manager drive the entire conversation
- Only talking about tasks and never about your growth
- Hiding problems until they become emergencies
- Canceling or rescheduling 1-on-1s repeatedly
- Treating it like a performance review (it is not)

## 1.4. Key Phrases for Understanding the Purpose

- "What's the best way to use our 1-on-1 time?"
- "I'd like to make sure I'm getting the most out of these meetings."
- "Is there anything you'd like me to prepare before our next 1-on-1?"
- "How do you prefer to structure our 1-on-1s — should I come with an agenda?"
- "I want to make sure these meetings are valuable for both of us."

# 2. PREPARING FOR YOUR 1-ON-1

## 2.1. Agenda Template

Always prepare a short agenda before the meeting. Send it 1-2 hours ahead or bring it as notes.

```
1-on-1 Agenda — [Date]
1. Quick updates (5 min)
   - [Project A] status
   - [Project B] status
2. Blockers / Help needed (5 min)
   - [Describe blocker]
3. Topics I'd like to discuss (10 min)
   - [Topic 1]
   - [Topic 2]
4. Feedback for me (5 min)
   - Anything I should improve?
5. Action items from last time
   - [Item 1] — done / in progress
```

## 2.2. Topics to Bring Up

- Wins and accomplishments you want your manager to know about
- Challenges or blockers you need help with
- Career development questions
- Feedback on team processes or tools
- Ideas for improving the product or codebase
- Concerns about workload or priorities

## 2.3. What to Avoid in 1-on-1s

- Complaining about colleagues without context or proposed solutions
- Asking questions you could answer by checking the wiki or Slack
- Spending the whole time on status updates instead of meaningful conversation
- Gossip or speculation about company politics

## 2.4. Phrases for Setting the Agenda

- "I put together a few topics I'd like to cover today. Can I share my screen?"
- "Before we start, here are the three things I wanted to discuss."
- "I have a quick update on [project], and then I'd like to talk about [bigger topic]."
- "Is there anything pressing on your end that we should address first?"
- "I sent over a short agenda earlier — should we follow that or would you like to adjust?"
- "I'd like to spend most of our time today on [topic], if that works for you."

# 3. REPORTING PROGRESS & CHALLENGES

## 3.1. Giving Status Updates

Keep updates concise. Focus on outcomes, not activities.

- "[Project] is on track. We finished the API integration and started on the frontend."
- "We hit a small delay on [feature] because of [reason], but we expect to catch up by [date]."
- "I completed [task] ahead of schedule, so I've started working on [next task]."
- "The main deliverable this week was [X]. It's been deployed to staging."
- "[Project] is about 70% complete. The remaining work is [describe]."

## 3.2. Raising Blockers and Challenges

Be direct but solution-oriented.

- "I'm blocked on [task] because I need access to [system]. Can you help me get that?"
- "We've run into a technical challenge with [feature]. I have two possible approaches I'd like your input on."
- "The deadline for [project] is tight given the current scope. Can we discuss prioritization?"
- "I'm waiting on a decision from the product team before I can proceed with [task]."
- "One risk I want to flag: [describe risk]. Here's what I think we should do about it."

## 3.3. Sharing Wins

Do not be modest — your manager may not see everything you do.

- "I wanted to share that we successfully launched [feature] with zero downtime."
- "The client gave positive feedback on the demo last week."
- "I refactored the authentication module and reduced load time by 40%."
- "I mentored [junior] through their first production deployment."
- "We closed all critical bugs from last sprint."

## 3.4. Phrases for Discussing Priorities

- "I have three things in progress. Which should I prioritize?"
- "I want to make sure I'm spending my time on the highest-impact work."
- "Given the new deadline, should I deprioritize [task]?"
- "Can we revisit the priority of [project]? I think [reason] may change its urgency."

# 4. DISCUSSING CAREER GROWTH

## 4.1. Asking About Promotion Paths

- "I'd like to understand what the path to senior developer looks like on our team."
- "What skills or experiences should I focus on to be considered for the next level?"
- "Can we create a development plan with specific milestones for my growth?"
- "What does a strong candidate for promotion look like in your eyes?"
- "Is there a competency framework I can review for the next level?"
- "How does the promotion process work on our team? When are decisions typically made?"

## 4.2. Expressing Career Ambitions

- "I'm really interested in growing toward a tech lead role. What steps should I take?"
- "I'd like to get more exposure to architecture decisions. Are there opportunities for that?"
- "I want to improve my communication skills, especially with stakeholders. Can you help me find opportunities?"
- "I enjoy mentoring and would like to take on more of that responsibility."
- "I'm interested in exploring [area] more deeply. Are there projects I could contribute to?"

## 4.3. Asking for Specific Opportunities

- "Could I lead the next sprint planning to build my facilitation skills?"
- "I'd like to present at the next team tech talk. Can I sign up?"
- "Is there a stretch assignment or cross-team project I could take on?"
- "I want to get better at system design. Could I shadow you during the next architecture review?"
- "Would it be possible for me to attend [conference/course] for professional development?"

## 4.4. Phrases for Receiving Career Feedback

- "What's one thing I could do in the next quarter that would have the biggest impact on my growth?"
- "Are there any gaps in my skills that I should work on?"
- "How am I perceived by the team and stakeholders? Any blind spots I should know about?"
- "What would make you confident in recommending me for promotion?"

# 5. REQUESTING SUPPORT & RESOURCES

## 5.1. How to Ask for Help Professionally

Frame your request around impact and outcomes, not personal need.

- "To deliver [project] on time, I'll need [resource]. Is that possible?"
- "I've identified a gap in our testing coverage. I'd like to propose we allocate one sprint to address it."
- "I think bringing in [tool/service] would save the team about [X] hours per week. Can we discuss the budget?"
- "The current [system/process] is causing repeated delays. I'd like to explore alternatives."
- "I'd benefit from some guidance on [topic]. Do you know someone who could help?"

## 5.2. Asking for More Time or People

- "The scope of [project] has grown significantly. I'd like to discuss whether we need to adjust the timeline or add resources."
- "Based on my analysis, we need an additional developer to meet the Q3 deadline. Here's the data."
- "I'm at full capacity with my current tasks. If [new request] is a priority, which of my other tasks should I pause?"
- "Would it be possible to get a contractor for [specific work] for the next two months?"

## 5.3. Asking for Training and Learning Resources

- "I found a course on [topic] that would directly help with our current project. The cost is [amount]. Would the company sponsor it?"
- "I'd like to dedicate a few hours each week to learning [skill]. Is there a policy for learning time?"
- "Are there any internal training programs or mentorship opportunities I should know about?"
- "I'd like to attend [event/conference]. Here's how it would benefit the team."

## 5.4. Phrases That Show Competence, Not Helplessness

- "I want to make sure I approach this the right way. Can I get your perspective?"
- "I've done my research and have a few options, but I'd value your experience on this."
- "This is a new area for me, and I want to ramp up quickly. What would you recommend?"
- "I could figure this out on my own, but I think your input would help me get there faster and with better results."

# 6. GIVING UPWARD FEEDBACK

## 6.1. Principles of Upward Feedback

Giving feedback to your manager can feel uncomfortable, especially in Vietnamese work culture where hierarchy is respected. However, most managers genuinely want to improve and appreciate thoughtful feedback.

Rules:
- Be specific, not vague
- Focus on behavior, not personality
- Use "I" statements instead of "You" statements
- Offer suggestions, not just criticism
- Choose the right moment (not during a crisis)

## 6.2. Feedback on Communication Style

- "I find it most helpful when context is shared in writing before meetings. Could we try that?"
- "I learn best when I get feedback close to when the work happens. Would it be possible to do quick async feedback instead of saving it for 1-on-1s?"
- "Sometimes I'm not sure about the priority of different tasks. A quick Slack message would help me stay aligned."
- "I appreciate when you explain the 'why' behind decisions. It helps me make better choices in my work."

## 6.3. Feedback on Meeting Management

- "I'd love to have more unstructured time in our 1-on-1s for career discussions. Could we keep status updates to the first 5 minutes?"
- "Sometimes our 1-on-1s get canceled when things get busy. Could we protect that time? It really helps me stay on track."
- "It would help me if we could have more consistent meeting times. I find it hard to prepare when meetings shift around."

## 6.4. Disagreeing Professionally

- "I see it differently. Can I share my perspective?"
- "I understand your reasoning. I have a concern about [specific point]. Mind if I walk through it?"
- "I want to push back gently on [decision]. Here's why I think a different approach might work better."
- "I respect your call on this. I just want to make sure we've considered [alternative]."
- "Let me play devil's advocate for a moment. What if [scenario]?"

# 7. HANDLING DIFFICULT TOPICS

## 7.1. Raising Issues with Teammates

Be factual and focus on impact, not personality.

- "I wanted to talk about a dynamic on the team that's affecting our velocity."
- "I'm having trouble collaborating with [person] on [project]. Here's what's been happening."
- "I want to handle this constructively. Do you have advice on how to approach the situation?"
- "I've already tried [approach], but the issue continues. I'd appreciate your guidance."
- "This is hard for me to bring up, but I think it's important for the team's success."

## 7.2. Discussing Workload Concerns

- "I want to be transparent: my current workload is not sustainable. I'm working late most nights."
- "I've taken on [X] additional responsibilities since [event]. I'd like to discuss rebalancing."
- "I'm concerned about burnout. Can we look at my task list together and see what can be deprioritized or delegated?"
- "I want to deliver high-quality work, and right now I'm spread too thin to do that consistently."

## 7.3. Talking About Burnout

- "I want to be honest — I've been feeling burned out lately. I think I need to make some changes."
- "I've lost some motivation recently. I think part of it is [reason]. Can we talk about it?"
- "I don't want to reach the point where I can't perform. I'd like to proactively address this."
- "I've been consistently working overtime for the past [period]. I need to reset to a sustainable pace."

## 7.4. Raising Ethical or Process Concerns

- "I noticed something in our process that concerns me. I'd like to discuss it privately."
- "I think we may be cutting corners on [quality/testing] in a way that could cause problems later."
- "I want to flag a potential risk with [decision]. I don't think the team has fully considered [impact]."
- "I'm not comfortable with [specific situation]. Can we talk about how to handle it?"

# 8. FOLLOWING UP AFTER 1-ON-1

## 8.1. Email Template: Meeting Summary

```
Subject: 1-on-1 Notes — [Date]

Hi [Manager Name],

Thanks for the discussion today. Here's a quick summary:

Topics discussed:
- [Topic 1]: [Brief summary]
- [Topic 2]: [Brief summary]

Action items:
- [ ] [Action item 1] — Owner: [name] — Due: [date]
- [ ] [Action item 2] — Owner: [name] — Due: [date]

Next steps:
- [Any follow-up items]

Let me know if I missed anything.

Best,
[Your Name]
```

## 8.2. Email Template: Follow-Up on a Request

```
Subject: Following Up — [Topic from 1-on-1]

Hi [Manager Name],

During our 1-on-1 on [date], we discussed [topic]. I wanted
to follow up and see if there are any updates.

As I mentioned, this would help me with [specific impact].

Happy to discuss further if needed.

Thanks,
[Your Name]
```

## 8.3. Tracking Action Items

- Keep a running document or note with action items from each 1-on-1
- Review the list before your next meeting
- Mark completed items and bring up delayed items proactively
- "I wanted to check in on the action item from last time regarding [topic]."

## 8.4. Phrases for Following Up

- "I wanted to circle back on something we discussed in our last 1-on-1."
- "Just checking in on [topic]. Has there been any progress?"
- "I completed [action item] from our last meeting. Here's a quick summary."
- "I haven't been able to make progress on [item] because [reason]. I wanted to flag that."

# 9. COMMON 1-ON-1 SCENARIOS WITH SCRIPTS

## Scenario 1: First 1-on-1 with a New Manager

**You:** "Thanks for making time for this. I'd love to learn more about your management style and what you expect from these meetings."

**Manager:** "Great. I like to use 1-on-1s to understand how you're doing and remove any blockers. What works best for you?"

**You:** "I appreciate that. I typically come with a short agenda — quick updates, then any blockers or topics I'd like to discuss. I also like to use this time for career conversations when possible. Does that format work for you?"

**Manager:** "That sounds good. What's top of mind for you right now?"

**You:** "I'd like to understand the team's priorities for this quarter so I can make sure my work is aligned. Also, I'm interested in taking on more ownership of [area]."

## Scenario 2: Asking for a Promotion

**You:** "I'd like to talk about my career path today. I've been in my current role for [time], and I'd like to understand what it would take to be considered for the next level."

**Manager:** "Sure, let's discuss. What makes you feel ready?"

**You:** "Over the past year, I've consistently delivered on my commitments. I led [project], mentored [junior], and improved [system]. I think I'm already performing at the next level in several areas."

**Manager:** "I agree you've been doing great work. Let me think about specific areas to develop."

**You:** "That would be really helpful. Could we create a development plan with clear milestones? I want to make sure I'm focusing on the right things."

## Scenario 3: Raising a Workload Concern

**You:** "I want to be transparent about my workload. I'm currently juggling four projects, and I'm starting to feel stretched thin."

**Manager:** "I appreciate you bringing this up. Which projects are taking the most time?"

**You:** "[Project A] and [Project B] both have tight deadlines this month. I've been working late to keep up, but I don't think it's sustainable. I'd like to discuss how we can rebalance."

**Manager:** "Let's look at priorities. Which of these is most critical?"

**You:** "Based on the business impact, [Project A] should be the top priority. If we can pause or hand off [Project C], I think I can deliver quality work on the other three."

## Scenario 4: Giving Feedback to Your Manager

**You:** "I'd like to share some feedback, if you're open to it. I want to make sure it's constructive."

**Manager:** "Of course, please go ahead."

**You:** "I've noticed that requirements sometimes change without much context. For example, last week the scope of [feature] shifted in the middle of the sprint. It would help me a lot if we could discuss changes like that before they happen, or at least get a written summary of what changed and why."

**Manager:** "That's fair. I'll try to be more proactive about communicating changes."

**You:** "Thanks, I really appreciate it. I'm happy to help however I can — even a quick Slack heads-up would make a big difference."

## Scenario 5: Discussing a Difficult Teammate

**You:** "I want to talk about a collaboration challenge. I want to handle this carefully and constructively."

**Manager:** "Sure, what's going on?"

**You:** "I've been working with [person] on [project], and I'm finding it difficult to get timely code reviews. I've asked a few times, but reviews often come back late, which blocks my progress."

**Manager:** "Have you tried talking to them directly?"

**You:** "Yes, I brought it up casually twice. They were apologetic, but the pattern continues. I don't want to escalate too formally — I'm hoping you might have advice on how to approach this."

**Manager:** "Let me have a word with them. I'll keep it low-key and frame it as a team process improvement."

**You:** "That would be great. I want to maintain a good working relationship with them."

## Scenario 6: Following Up on a Previous Discussion

**You:** "In our last 1-on-1, we talked about me getting more exposure to system design discussions. I wanted to follow up on that."

**Manager:** "Right, I was looking into that. Let me check where things stand."

**You:** "I saw there's an architecture review coming up for [project]. Would it be possible for me to sit in on that? I think it would be a great learning opportunity."

**Manager:** "That's a good idea. Let me add you to the invite."

**You:** "Thanks. I'll prepare by reviewing the existing design docs beforehand so I can follow the discussion better."

## Scenario 7: Asking for Training Budget

**You:** "I'd like to discuss professional development. I found a course on [topic] that I believe would directly benefit our team."

**Manager:** "Sure, tell me more about it."

**You:** "It covers [specific skills] that we're currently missing on the team. The cost is [amount], and it's self-paced so it won't interfere with my project work. I'd like to request the company sponsor it."

**Manager:** "That sounds reasonable. Send me the details and I'll run it by finance."

**You:** "Will do. I'll also put together a short summary of how I plan to apply what I learn to our current projects."

# CONCLUSION

1-on-1 meetings with management are your best tool for career growth, getting support, and building a strong professional relationship. Remember:

- **Prepare every time.** A 5-minute agenda transforms the meeting.
- **Be honest about challenges.** Managers cannot help with problems they do not know about.
- **Own your career.** No one will advocate for you more effectively than you.
- **Follow up in writing.** Document decisions and track action items.
- **Build trust over time.** Consistent, constructive communication earns your manager's confidence.

The more you practice these conversations, the more natural they will feel. Start small — bring one topic to your next 1-on-1 that you would not normally raise.
