# SALARY NEGOTIATION

## Scripts, templates, and strategies for negotiating your compensation package professionally

> **Purpose of this document**
>
> - Understand your market value and how to research salary benchmarks
> - Learn when and how to negotiate using email templates and verbal scripts
> - Master 10+ complete negotiation scripts for different scenarios
> - Handle common pushback like "budget is tight" or "we don't negotiate"
> - Negotiate beyond base salary: equity, bonuses, remote work, vacation, and more

# 1. UNDERSTANDING YOUR MARKET VALUE

Before you negotiate, you need to know what you're worth. Research prevents you from lowballing yourself or asking for unrealistic numbers.

## 1.1. Research Phrases

1. "What is the typical salary range for a Senior Software Engineer with 5 years of experience in [city/region]?"
2. "Could you share the salary band for this role so I can set realistic expectations?"
3. "I've been researching market rates for similar roles in this region. What range did you have in mind?"
4. "Is the compensation for this role aligned with the mid-to-senior range for the local market?"
5. "What does the total compensation package look like? I want to understand the full picture beyond just base salary."

## 1.2. Where to Research

- **Glassdoor, Levels.fyi, Blind**: Check salary reports for the specific company and role.
- **LinkedIn Salary**: Filter by location, experience, and company size.
- **Stack Overflow Developer Survey**: Annual benchmark for developer salaries worldwide.
- **Robert Half Technology Salary Guide**: Regional salary ranges by role and seniority.
- **Local tech communities**: Ask peers in Vietnam tech groups (VietnamWorks, TopDev) about current rates.

## 1.3. Phrases for Researching with Recruiters

1. "Before we discuss numbers, could you share the budgeted range for this position?"
2. "I want to make sure we're aligned on compensation expectations. What range is the company targeting for this role?"
3. "Could you walk me through the full compensation structure? I'd like to understand base, bonus, equity, and benefits."
4. "Is there a standard compensation band for this level, or is it evaluated case by case?"

# 2. WHEN AND HOW TO NEGOTIATE

Timing matters. Negotiate at the right moment and with the right approach.

## 2.1. Best Times to Negotiate

1. **After receiving a written offer**: You have the most leverage because the company has already decided they want you.
2. **During the final interview when asked about salary expectations**: Give a range, not a single number.
3. **When you have a competing offer**: Use it as leverage but stay professional.
4. **Never negotiate salary during the first interview** unless the recruiter brings it up. Focus on demonstrating your value first.

## 2.2. How to Start the Conversation

1. "Thank you for the offer. I'm very excited about the opportunity. I'd like to discuss the compensation package if you're open to it."
2. "I've reviewed the offer letter and I'm thrilled about the role. There are a couple of points I'd love to explore around compensation."
3. "I appreciate the offer and I'm very interested in joining the team. I was hoping we could have a conversation about the salary to see if we can find a number that works for both of us."
4. "Thank you for extending the offer. Before I make my final decision, I'd like to discuss the compensation to make sure it aligns with my experience and the market."

## 2.3. Email Template: Opening a Negotiation

```
Subject: Re: Offer Letter - [Your Name] - [Role Title]

Hi [Recruiter Name],

Thank you so much for the offer. I'm genuinely excited about the
opportunity to join [Company] and contribute to the [team/project].

After reviewing the offer details, I'd love to have a brief
conversation about the compensation package. I want to make sure
we find terms that reflect my experience and the value I'll bring
to the team.

Would you be available for a quick call this week?

Best regards,
[Your Name]
```

# 3. RESPONDING TO INITIAL OFFERS

When you receive an offer, you have three options: accept, counter, or ask for more time.

## 3.1. Accepting the Offer

1. "Thank you for the offer. The compensation package meets my expectations, and I'm happy to accept."
2. "I'm thrilled with the offer. The salary and benefits are exactly what I was hoping for. When can I sign the contract?"
3. "This looks great. I'm excited to join the team and I'll sign the offer letter today."

## 3.2. Countering the Offer

1. "Thank you for the offer. Based on my research and experience, I was hoping for a base salary closer to [amount]. Is there room to discuss?"
2. "I appreciate the offer. Given my [X] years of experience in [technology/skill] and the market rates for this role, I'd like to propose a base salary of [amount]."
3. "The role and the team are exactly what I'm looking for. The salary is a bit below my expectations though. Could we explore a range around [amount]?"
4. "I'm very interested in this position. The current offer is about [X]% below what I was targeting. Would it be possible to close that gap?"

## 3.3. Asking for Time

1. "Thank you for the offer. I'd like to take a day or two to review everything carefully before giving you my final answer."
2. "I'm very excited about this opportunity. Could I get back to you by [specific date] with my decision?"
3. "I want to make an informed decision. Would it be okay if I review the offer details and respond by end of day tomorrow?"
4. "I appreciate the offer and I'm seriously considering it. I'd like to discuss this with my family and get back to you by [day]. Is that okay?"

# 4. NEGOTIATION SCRIPTS & TEMPLATES

Here are 10+ complete scripts for the most common negotiation scenarios.

## Script 1: Initial Offer Is Too Low

> "Thank you for the offer. I'm genuinely excited about the opportunity to join [Company] and work on [specific project or team]. After reviewing the compensation, I was hoping for a base salary closer to [target amount]. Based on my [X] years of experience with [specific skills] and the current market rates, I believe [target amount] is a fair reflection of the value I'd bring. Is there flexibility to move closer to that number?"

## Script 2: You Have a Competing Offer

> "I want to be transparent with you. I have another offer on the table for [amount], but I'm much more excited about the work your team is doing at [Company]. If you can match or come close to [amount], I'd sign your offer today. Would that be possible?"

## Script 3: Asking for Equity Review

> "Thank you for the offer. The base salary is in the right range, but I'd like to discuss the equity component. Based on what I've seen for similar roles at companies of this stage and size, the current equity grant seems a bit below market. Could we discuss increasing the stock options to [target number of shares or percentage]?"

## Script 4: Requesting Remote Work as a Benefit

> "I'm very happy with the compensation package. One thing I'd like to discuss is the work arrangement. I'd love to work remotely [full-time / 3 days a week] from [city]. This would actually save the company on office costs, and I have a proven track record of being productive in remote settings. Would that be something you'd consider?"

## Script 5: Negotiating After a Promotion

> "I've really enjoyed taking on the additional responsibilities of a [new role] over the past [time period]. I've led [specific project] and delivered [specific result]. I'd like to discuss adjusting my compensation to reflect this new level of responsibility. I was thinking of a base salary increase to [target amount]. Can we schedule a time to discuss this?"

## Script 6: Counter-Offer When Company Says Budget Is Fixed

> "I completely understand that the base salary is capped at that level. Could we explore other ways to close the gap? For instance, a sign-on bonus, additional stock options, or an extra week of vacation would make the overall package very attractive to me."

## Script 7: Negotiating as a Contractor-to-Full-Time Conversion

> "I've been working as a contractor on this project for [time period], and I'm thrilled about the opportunity to join full-time. During my time here, I've [specific achievements]. Given my deep knowledge of the codebase and the team's processes, I'd like to propose a starting salary of [amount], which reflects the ramp-up time you'd save by converting me versus hiring someone new."

## Script 8: Asking for a Sign-On Bonus

> "I'm excited about the offer. I do have one request: would it be possible to include a sign-on bonus of [amount]? This would help cover [relocation costs / the bonus I'm forfeiting at my current employer / transition expenses] and would make the decision to join much easier."

## Script 9: Re-Negotiating a Year Later (Annual Review)

> "Over the past year, I've taken on [specific additional responsibilities] and delivered [specific results]. I believe my contributions have exceeded the expectations of my current role. I'd like to discuss a compensation adjustment that reflects the increased scope of my work. Based on my research, a salary of [amount] would be aligned with the market for someone in this role with my level of impact."

## Script 10: Turning Down an Offer Politely Due to Compensation

> "Thank you for the generous offer. I've thoroughly enjoyed the interview process and have a lot of respect for the team. However, after careful consideration, the compensation doesn't quite align with my current needs and market expectations. I'd love to stay in touch and hope our paths cross again in the future."

## Script 11: When They Ask for Your Salary History

> "I prefer not to share my current salary as I'd like our discussion to be based on the value I bring to this role and the market rate for the position. Based on my research, I believe a fair range for this role is [range]. Does that align with your budget?"

# 5. HANDLING PUSHBACK

Recruiters and hiring managers may resist. Here are responses to the most common objections.

## 5.1. "The Budget Is Tight Right Now"

1. "I understand budget constraints are real. Could we explore a sign-on bonus or a performance-based bonus to bridge the gap?"
2. "I appreciate the transparency. Would it be possible to schedule a salary review in 6 months instead of waiting for the annual cycle?"
3. "That's completely understandable. Could we look at other aspects of the package, like additional PTO, a professional development budget, or remote work flexibility?"

## 5.2. "We Don't Negotiate with New Hires"

1. "I respect the company's policy. Could you help me understand the full compensation structure so I can evaluate the total package?"
2. "That's fair. Is there any flexibility on the start date, title, or team assignment that might make the offer more attractive?"
3. "I appreciate the clarity. Would it be possible to have a guaranteed salary review after my first 6 months, tied to specific performance milestones?"

## 5.3. "This Is Our Best Offer"

1. "Thank you for being upfront. Let me take a day to review the entire package and get back to you with my final decision."
2. "I appreciate the best offer. Would you be open to reviewing the equity component or adding a sign-on bonus?"
3. "I understand. The total package is [close to / below] what I need. Let me think this over carefully and respond by [date]."

## 5.4. "We Can Do [Small Increase]. Take It or Leave It."

1. "Thank you for the adjustment. I'd like to take 24 hours to consider this before making my final decision."
2. "I appreciate the effort to move. If we could add [specific benefit, e.g., extra week of PTO / sign-on bonus], I'd be happy to sign today."
3. "Thank you. Could we also discuss the possibility of a 6-month salary review to revisit this conversation once I've proven my value on the team?"

# 6. NEGOTIATING BEYOND BASE SALARY

If the base salary is fixed, there are many other levers to pull. The total compensation package includes much more than just base pay.

## 6.1. Benefits & Perks

1. "Could you tell me more about the health insurance coverage? Does it include dental and vision?"
2. "Is there a professional development budget? I'd love to attend conferences and take online courses."
3. "Does the company offer a home office stipend for remote employees?"
4. "What's the parental leave policy? I'd like to understand the full benefits package."

## 6.2. Bonuses

1. "Could you share details about the annual performance bonus? What's the target percentage and how is it calculated?"
2. "Is there a sign-on bonus available? That would help bridge the gap between my current offer and my target."
3. "Are there any referral bonuses or project completion bonuses?"

## 6.3. Equity & Stock Options

1. "Could you walk me through the equity component? What's the vesting schedule and the current valuation?"
2. "Is there an ESPP (Employee Stock Purchase Plan)? If so, what's the discount?"
3. "How many stock options does this offer include, and what's the strike price?"
4. "What was the company's most recent valuation, and how does the equity offer compare to similar roles?"

## 6.4. Vacation & Time Off

1. "How many PTO days are included in the offer? Is there flexibility to negotiate additional days?"
2. "Does the company offer unlimited PTO, or is there a set number of days?"
3. "Would it be possible to start with 3 weeks of vacation instead of the standard 2 weeks?"
4. "Are there company-wide shutdown periods or flexible holidays?"

## 6.5. Remote Work & Flexibility

1. "Is this role fully remote, hybrid, or on-site? What's the expected in-office cadence?"
2. "I'd like to work remotely from Vietnam. Is the company open to hiring internationally?"
3. "Could we agree on a flexible schedule? I work best when I can align my hours with both the team and my family time zone."
4. "Does the company offer a co-working space stipend for remote employees?"

## 6.6. Professional Development

1. "Does the company provide a budget for conferences and certifications?"
2. "Is there a formal mentorship program or career development plan?"
3. "Would the company support me in obtaining an AWS certification or similar credentials?"

# 7. WRITTEN FOLLOW-UP TEMPLATES

Always follow up a verbal negotiation with a written summary. This creates a paper trail and ensures both sides are aligned.

## 7.1. After a Successful Negotiation

```
Subject: Re: Updated Offer - [Your Name]

Hi [Recruiter Name],

Thank you for the great conversation today. I'm happy to confirm
that I accept the updated offer with the following terms:

- Base salary: [amount]
- Sign-on bonus: [amount]
- Annual performance bonus target: [X]% of base salary
- Equity: [number] stock options with a [X]-year vesting schedule
- Start date: [date]

I'm very excited to join [Company] and look forward to contributing
to the [team/project]. Please let me know if there's anything else
you need from me before my start date.

Best regards,
[Your Name]
```

## 7.2. After a Verbal Counter-Offer

```
Subject: Re: Compensation Discussion - [Your Name]

Hi [Recruiter Name],

Thank you for taking the time to discuss the offer with me today.
As we discussed, I'm requesting a base salary of [amount],
which I believe is in line with my experience and market rates.

To summarize our conversation:
- Current offer: [amount]
- My counter-offer: [amount]
- Reasoning: [X] years of experience in [skill], proven track
  record of [achievement], and market benchmarking data

I remain very enthusiastic about the role and would love to find
terms that work for both sides. Please let me know your thoughts.

Best regards,
[Your Name]
```

## 7.3. Declining an Offer Due to Compensation

```
Subject: Re: Offer Letter - [Your Name]

Hi [Recruiter Name],

Thank you again for the offer and for the time you spent with me
throughout the interview process. I genuinely enjoyed meeting the
team and learning about the exciting work at [Company].

After careful consideration, I've decided to decline the offer.
While I'm very enthusiastic about the role and the team, the
compensation package doesn't align with my current financial
needs and market expectations.

I'd love to stay connected and hope our paths cross again in the
future. Wishing you and the team all the best.

Best regards,
[Your Name]
```

# 8. COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID

Vietnamese engineers negotiating with Western companies should be aware of these cultural pitfalls.

## 8.1. Cultural Tips for Vietnamese Engineers

1. **Don't apologize for negotiating.** In Western work culture, negotiation is expected and respected. Never say "Sorry to ask, but..." or "I don't mean to be greedy."
2. **Don't accept the first offer immediately.** Even if the first offer is good, take time to review it. A quick "yes" can signal that you undervalue yourself.
3. **Don't share your current salary.** Many Western companies have banned salary history questions. Focus on market value, not past pay.
4. **Don't make it personal.** Keep the conversation professional. Focus on data, market rates, and the value you bring -- not personal financial needs.
5. **Don't bluff about competing offers.** If you say you have another offer, be prepared to prove it or lose credibility entirely.
6. **Don't negotiate via chat or text message.** Use a phone call or video call for the main discussion, followed by a written email summary.
7. **Don't focus only on base salary.** Western companies often have significant value in equity, bonuses, and benefits. Look at total compensation.
8. **Don't be afraid to walk away.** The strongest negotiating position is being genuinely willing to say no. If the offer doesn't meet your minimum, declining politely is better than accepting an offer you'll regret.

## 8.2. Phrases to Avoid

1. ~~"I'm sorry to ask for more money."~~ -> "I'd like to discuss the compensation to ensure it reflects the value I bring."
2. ~~"I'll take whatever you offer."~~ -> "I'd like to review the full package before making a decision."
3. ~~"I really need this job."~~ -> "I'm very excited about this opportunity and want to find terms that work for both of us."
4. ~~"My current salary is [amount]."~~ -> "Based on my research, the market rate for this role is [range]."
5. ~~"That's fine, I guess."~~ -> "Let me take some time to review the offer and get back to you."

## 8.3. Red Flags to Watch For

1. The recruiter pressures you to accept immediately: "This offer expires in 24 hours." (Legitimate offers give you at least a few days.)
2. They refuse to put the offer in writing. (Always get a written offer before accepting.)
3. They ask for your current pay stubs or tax returns. (This is illegal in many jurisdictions.)
4. The equity offer has no clear vesting schedule or valuation. (Ask for clarification before signing.)

# CONCLUSION

Salary negotiation is a skill that improves with practice. Remember these key principles:

- **Do your homework.** Research market rates before every negotiation so you have data to back up your request.
- **Always express enthusiasm first.** Start every negotiation by affirming your excitement about the role and the company.
- **Think in total compensation.** Base salary, equity, bonuses, benefits, remote work, and vacation are all negotiable.
- **Get everything in writing.** After any verbal agreement, send a follow-up email summarizing the terms.
- **It's OK to walk away.** Declining an offer that doesn't meet your needs is better than accepting one you'll regret.
- **Practice the scripts out loud.** The more you rehearse, the more natural and confident you'll sound during the real conversation.
- **Don't apologize for knowing your worth.** Western companies expect negotiation and respect candidates who advocate for themselves professionally.
